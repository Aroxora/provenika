**The wedge, from first principles.** Every AI tool in drug discovery asserts. Provenika's rule is *compute or cite, never assert*: every figure is either deterministically computed or fetched-and-cited from public sources (ChEMBL, UniProt, PDB, PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov), and a one-command verifier re-pulls each number from origin and returns PASS / DRIFT / FAIL. The output isn't an answer — it's a **re-verifiable proof object**.

**Why that's structurally defensible without a data moat:**
- *Trust compounds, reasoning doesn't.* A black-box score depreciates the instant the next model ships. A receipt that anyone can independently re-run *gains* credibility every time someone checks it and it holds. That's a one-way ratchet — the asset incumbents (Schrödinger, Insilico, Causaly, BenchSci, even Open Targets) structurally cannot copy, because auditability isn't a feature you bolt on; it constrains the whole architecture.
- *Brand-as-moat in open source.* Open-sourcing the engine *is* the distribution and the credibility flywheel — the public verifier turns every skeptical scientist into a free auditor and the standard becomes "Provenika-checked." Linux/HuggingFace dynamics, in a field that runs on citations.
- *The honest weaknesses are the point.* All sources are public (no data moat). Open Targets gives the headline lookup away free. This is **research-only — not a cure, drug, or medical-advice company.** The moat is the verification standard layered *on top of* free data, not the data.
